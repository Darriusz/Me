Cascading

sound has the property of being all around us. A book is enclosed in a small object. A sculpture stands in front of us and the most we can do is go around it, never getting inside. A flat painting is only to be viewed from a distance. All of these are “the other”—objects separated from us.

However sounds swirl in the air about us, they constitute the space in which we live, move, and exist, and so there may even be more magic in them than in penetration of shapes and colors. To form such all-encompassing cascades is a most extraordinary, absolutely ravishing experience, a total stepping out of linguistically structured domain of the signified into a space of amorphous being. It’s a kind of primeval esthetic-emotional experience outside the realm of tracking down meanings in black and white.

From a neuroscientific standpoint it may be simply an activation of the brain areas independent of language processing (and as such it gives a superb repose after long hours of text chores which often leave odd phrases rattling in the mind on end). Granted, the neural firing must come from auditory areas (like in speech reception) but the subsequent elaboration in the cortex appears to evade typical paths of language processing. And since I’m started on it, neuroscience will surely allow us to grasp the neural mapping of the outside world some day and one might imagine a subtle art pharmaceutics: bringing about experiences that currently only arise due to, say, music by targeted chemical agents. (Our contemporary ersatz of consciousness altering drugs is more of a battering ram than plucking a cither gently.) The Fifth Symphony in pills or drops… But then there well may never be sufficient funds for such chemical-artistic extravagancies.

For the time being we are left with the old ear-method of digesting the sonic form of music. At this point let me explain the mp3 files provided above do not come from actual performances but are rather generated by a music notation software which has the sheet music in it. As a result they can only give a general taste of the development of the compositions (I recommend listening through the headphones).
